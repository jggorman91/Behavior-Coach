All behavior is a form of communication that serves a purpose for the individual. The four primary functions are Sensory, Escape, Attention, and Tangibles. Understanding the function of the behavior, not just what it looks like, must guide the intervention strategy.

Logical consequences are a non-punitive way to respond to misbehavior that helps students learn from their mistakes while preserving their dignity. To be logical, a consequence must be Related, Respectful, Reasonable, and Helpful. The three types are:

  • You Break It, You Fix It: The student is responsible for fixing the problem they caused, whether it was accidental or intentional.
  • Loss of Privilege: This is used when a student's behavior shows they are not ready for the responsibility that comes with a particular privilege. The consequence is the temporary and directly related loss of that privilege.
  • Positive Time-Out: This is used when a student is beginning to lose self-control and needs a brief time and space to calm down and regroup before rejoining the group.

When a student's behavior is escalating, the most important factor is the adult's response. The adult's calm, non-anxious presence is the primary de-escalation tool. A student in this state is operating from their emotional "downstairs brain," and their "upstairs brain" for logic is essentially offline. Key strategies include:

  • Use a Calm Tone: Speak slowly and use a low, monotonous tone of voice.
  • Validate Feelings, Not Behavior: Acknowledge the student's emotion without condoning the action with phrases like, "I can see you are very angry right now".
  • Give Space: Do not invade the student's personal space, as this can be perceived as threatening. Stand at an angle rather than face-to-face.
  • Offer Choices: Giving simple, controlled choices can restore a sense of agency and reduce power struggles.

Strategies are based on the principles in "From Disruption to Development: A Guide to Understanding and Managing Behavior in the K-5 Classroom."
