All behavior is a form of communication that serves a purpose. [cite_start]The four primary functions are Sensory, Escape, Attention, and Tangibles[cite: 46]. [cite_start]Understanding the function is key to choosing the right intervention[cite: 61].

[cite_start]

Logical consequences help students learn from mistakes while preserving dignity[cite: 319]. [cite_start]They should be Related, Respectful, Reasonable, and Helpful[cite: 320]. The three types are:

    [cite_start]
  • You Break It, You Fix It: The student is responsible for fixing the problem they caused (e.g., cleaning a spill)[cite: 327, 328].
    • [cite_start]
  • Loss of Privilege: A temporary loss of a privilege directly related to the misbehavior (e.g., losing art supply privileges if used destructively)[cite: 331, 332].
    • [cite_start]
  • Positive Time-Out: A brief time for a student to regroup in a calm-down space before rejoining the group[cite: 337].
[cite_start]

When a student is escalating, the adult's calm presence is the primary de-escalation tool[cite: 284]. [cite_start]Their rational brain is "offline"[cite: 286]. Key strategies include:

    [cite_start]
  • Use a Calm Tone: Speak slowly and in a low voice[cite: 293].
    • [cite_start]
  • Validate Feelings, Not Behavior: "I can see you are very angry right now."[cite: 295, 296].
    • [cite_start]
  • Give Space: Do not invade personal space; stand at an angle[cite: 307, 309].
    • [cite_start]
  • Offer Choices: Give simple, controlled choices to restore a sense of agency[cite: 303].

Strategies are based on the principles in "From Disruption to Development: A Guide to Understanding and Managing Behavior in the K-5 Classroom."
